The Flint Water Crisis

A public health disaster caused by infrastructure failure, chemical corrosion, and government decisions.

What Happened Timeline Chemistry Impact Future Solutions

What Happened

In 2014 the city of Flint, Michigan switched its drinking water source to the Flint River in order to save money during a financial emergency.

However, the river water was not treated with corrosion-control chemicals. Flint had thousands of old lead pipes, and the untreated water caused those pipes to corrode.

As the pipes corroded, toxic lead dissolved into the drinking water of nearly 100,000 residents.

Timeline of the Crisis

April 2014

Flint switches its water source from Lake Huron to the Flint River.

2014–2015

Residents complain about brown water, strange smell, and health symptoms.

September 2015

Researchers confirm dangerous levels of lead in the drinking water.

October 2015

The city switches back to treated Lake Huron water.

2016

A federal state of emergency is declared.

The Chemistry of Lead Contamination

Pipe Corrosion

Lead pipes normally develop a protective mineral coating. Without corrosion inhibitors, acidic or untreated water dissolves that coating and releases lead ions into water.

Missing Chemical Protection

Water systems normally add orthophosphate to prevent corrosion. Flint's treatment plant failed to add this chemical.

Impact on Residents

  • Nearly 100,000 residents exposed to contaminated water
  • Thousands of children exposed to lead
  • Lead poisoning affects brain development and nervous systems
  • Outbreak of Legionnaires' disease killed at least 12 people

How We Can Prevent Future Crises

Replace Lead Pipes

Millions of old lead pipes across the U.S. need replacement.

Better Water Treatment

Corrosion-control chemicals must be required and monitored.

Government Transparency

Officials must respond to community concerns quickly.

Infrastructure Investment

The U.S. needs major investment in water systems.
